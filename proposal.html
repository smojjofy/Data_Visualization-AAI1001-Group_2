1. Introduction and Project Overview

1.1 Our Project’s Aim

Southeast Asia’s rapid economic development has triggered a profound transformation in its regional energy landscape over the past few decades. Rising energy costs, volatile fuel markets, and changing post-COVID grid demands now sit at the center of intense public and macroeconomic debates, as member nations struggle to balance aggressive industrial growth, urban expansion, and household cost-of-living stability. However, aggregate regional statistics often obscure the vast structural disparities and distinct economic vulnerabilities built into each nation’s unique energy framework.

Our project seeks to answer three interrelated questions using the International Energy Agency’s (IEA) 2024 final energy consumption data for Southeast Asia.

First, how do final energy consumption mixes by fuel type actually vary across the different ASEAN nations, and what do these distinct portfolios reveal about each country’s baseline resource dependencies?

Second, are there meaningful structural disparities in how energy demand is distributed across end-use sectors that aggregate regional narratives conceal? The original visualization attempts to depict both fuel types and sector demands simultaneously, but its crowded layout, uniform column spacing, and ambiguous visual proximity make it incredibly difficult to isolate and evaluate these two separate dimensions.

Third, does the relationship between a country’s primary fuel reliance and its dominant end-use sector support or challenge the narrative that developing, agrarian-industrial economies face fundamentally different inflationary energy risks than hyper-dense, import-dependent commercial hubs?

Our improved chart aims to answer all three questions in a single visualization by encoding four dimensions simultaneously: country, data dimension (fuel profile vs. sector demand), consumption category, and percentage share. The redesign moves beyond the original chart’s cluttered, ambiguous layout to offer a clean, side-by-side comparative architecture. This framework reveals exactly where regional energy vulnerabilities are concentrated, how national fuel portfolios align with sectoral demands, and how diverse the structural economic pressures truly are across the holistic Southeast Asian landscape.

1.2.1 Visualization Structure and Design

The visualization selected is a dual-panel, 100% stacked column chart titled “Total final consumption mix by fuel and end-use sector in Southeast Asia, 2024”. The graphic functions as a regional comparative matrix, mapping the Total Final Consumption (TFC) of energy across the ten ASEAN member states. Nominally, the chart attempts to illustrate the region’s energy architecture simultaneously across three core dimensions:

  • Geographic Entity (X-Axis): Ten Southeast Asian nations arranged in an identical left-to-right sequence across both panels to facilitate parallel visual scanning.
  • Energy Supply Portfolio (Left Panel - “Fuels”): Displays the relative percentage share of primary energy carriers fueling each economy.
  • Sectoral Demand Profile (Right Panel - “Sector”): Tracks the percentage distribution of final energy consumption across functional economic sectors.

The chart is structurally divided into two horizontally juxtaposed panels sharing a unified vertical scale (0% to 100%). However, the immediate and most glaring design flaw of this visualization is the complete absence of an on-chart legend or keymap. While the vertical columns are neatly segmented into distinct color blocks, the graphic itself provides no native mechanism to decode what specific energy source or sectoral demand each color represents.

Consequently, the identification of the specific data categories utilized in this study—such as mapping the left panel’s color bands to Oil, Natural Gas, Coal, Bioenergy, and Electricity, and the right panel’s bands to Industry, Transport, Buildings, and Other—was only made possible by extracting supplementary data tables, text descriptions, and interactive metadata embedded deeper within the parent IEA report source.

By normalizing all columns to a uniform 100% height, the structural design prioritizes part-to-whole composition over absolute volumetric scale. This allows for a direct comparison of the fundamental energy “DNA” of highly diverse economies across a shared baseline, provided the viewer leverages external report documentation to overcome the chart’s native legibility bottleneck.

1.2.2 The Story It Tells

Despite the structural bottleneck caused by the missing on-chart legend, the underlying data framework remains a highly effective narrative tool when contextualized within the broader scope of the publication. By pairing this visualization with the surrounding report analysis and metadata, the IEA successfully leverages this dual-panel composition to tell a cohesive story of how energy types, costs, and demand vectors are structurally distributed across Southeast Asia. When decoded, the chart effectively dismantles the notion of a uniform regional energy market, revealing a landscape defined by sharp macroeconomic variations. Several distinct narratives emerge from the part-to-whole composition of the columns:

  1. The Shared Anchor of Petroleum: Across almost all ASEAN nations, Oil (represented by the dominant red base on the left panel) serves as the single largest bedrock fuel. This visual weight directly correlates with the Transport sector (orange blocks on the right panel), showing that despite varying levels of economic development, the region remains locked into liquid fossil fuel dependency to move goods and people.
  2. The Agrarian-to-Urban Development Divide: A stark contrast appears when tracking Bioenergy (teal bands) against Electricity (light blue bands). Developing or resource-rich continental economies display large blocks of bioenergy, often used for traditional building or residential heating and cooking. Conversely, highly urbanized, industrialized, or island economies show virtually zero bioenergy reliance, replacing it entirely with centralized Electricity and Natural Gas.
  3. Diverse Sectoral Engines: The right panel exposes fundamentally different economic drivers across the region. Certain nations stand out as industrial heavyweights, with their yellow “Industry” bars consuming nearly half of their total energy footprint. In contrast, other member states exhibit building-centric profiles, where massive green blocks reveal that the residential and commercial building footprint dwarfs the industrial and transport sectors combined.
  4. The Exogenous Shock Vulnerability Matrix: By reading the panels in parallel, the visualization tells a story of systemic risk. Nations displaying a simultaneous combination of high oil dependence (left) and massive transport/building sector exposure (right) are revealed to be highly vulnerable to external supply chain disruptions. Because their energy is spent on daily domestic survival and basic mobility rather than flexible industrial output, global energy price shocks translate directly into immediate domestic cost-of-living inflation.

2. Critical Analysis of Original Visualization

The original IEA visualization, Total final consumption mix by fuel and end-use sector in Southeast Asia, 2024, provides a compact overview of how energy consumption differs across Southeast Asian countries. Its main strength is that it compares two related dimensions in one figure: the fuel mix of each country and the end-use sector where energy is consumed.

However, the chart also has several limitations. While it works well as a broad regional overview, it is less effective for detailed comparison. The critique below focuses on three main areas: clarity, comparability, and visual design.

2.1 Clarity and Cognitive Load

The chart attempts to show two different analytical dimensions at the same time.

The fuel panel answers the question: what type of energy is consumed? This includes categories such as oil, coal, natural gas, bioenergy, electricity, and other fuels.

The sector panel answers a different question: where is energy used? This includes categories such as industry, transport, buildings, and other uses.

Although these two dimensions are related, they are not the same. Placing them side by side in one dense visual forces the reader to switch between two different category systems. This increases cognitive load, especially for readers who are not already familiar with energy terminology.

Another limitation is that the chart is based on percentage shares. This is useful for comparing composition, but it does not show the absolute volume of energy consumed by each country. As a result, a large energy consumer and a smaller energy consumer are given equal visual weight. This makes the chart effective for showing relative mix, but weaker for communicating the overall scale of regional energy demand.

2.2 Comparability Challenges

The stacked bar format makes some comparisons difficult. In a stacked bar chart, only the first segment starts from a common baseline. The remaining segments begin at different positions depending on the categories below them. This makes it harder to compare categories that are not aligned to the same starting point.

For example, if a reader wants to compare the share of coal, electricity, or bioenergy across several countries, they must visually estimate segment lengths from different starting positions. This makes the chart useful for a general overview, but less effective for precise cross-country comparison.

The chart also contains many countries and categories. Even though the original ordering provides regional context, it may not support every analytical question. If the goal is to compare fossil fuel reliance, electricity share, or industrial energy demand, sorting countries by a relevant metric would make the pattern clearer.

2.3 Visual Design and Accessibility

The chart relies heavily on colour to distinguish categories. This is necessary for a stacked composition chart, but it also creates visual complexity. When many colours are used in a small space, the chart becomes harder to read, especially if some colours are visually similar or if the reader has colour vision deficiencies.

The legend also requires frequent back-and-forth reading. Readers must look at the segment colour, check the legend, return to the chart, and then compare the segment across countries. This slows down interpretation and reduces the chart’s ability to communicate key insights quickly.

Finally, the chart has limited annotation. It shows many patterns, but it does not guide the reader toward the most important ones. For a report or presentation, the visualization would be stronger if it included clearer labels, short callouts, or a sorted structure that highlights the main story.

3. Proposed Improvements

Based on the critique above, our proposed redesign aims to preserve the original chart’s regional comparison purpose while making the visualization clearer, more accessible, and more useful for analysis.

The guiding question for our redesign is:

How do Southeast Asian countries differ in what energy they consume, and where that energy is ultimately used?

Instead of simply making the original chart look more attractive, our improvements focus on helping the reader compare countries more accurately and understand the main energy patterns more quickly.

3.2 Redesign Direction

Our main redesign will use a horizontal two-panel 100% stacked bar chart. The left panel will show each country’s fuel mix, while the right panel will show each country’s end-use sector mix.

This keeps the strongest idea of the original visualization: comparing countries across both energy source and energy use. However, placing countries on the y-axis improves readability because country names no longer need to be rotated or squeezed under the chart.

The two-panel structure also makes the logic clearer:

  • Fuel Mix answers: What type of energy is consumed?
  • End-use Sector answers: Where is energy used?

This makes the chart easier to interpret while still preserving the original report’s regional comparison purpose.

3.3 Sorting and Comparison

The original chart is useful for giving a broad regional overview, but different research questions require different ordering. In our redesign, we propose sorting countries based on the analytical story being told.

For example:

  • If the story is about fossil fuel dependence, countries can be sorted by fossil fuel share.
  • If the story is about electrification, countries can be sorted by electricity share.
  • If the story is about industrial energy demand, countries can be sorted by industry share.

This turns the chart from a static overview into a more analytical comparison. The ordering itself helps reveal the pattern.

3.4 Colour and Accessibility

The original visualization uses many colours across two panels, which makes the chart visually busy. Our redesign will use a more controlled colour strategy.

Fuel categories and sector categories should remain visually distinct. Colours should also be readable for users with colour vision deficiencies. This is important because colour is the main encoding method in a stacked composition chart. If colours are difficult to distinguish, the chart becomes harder to understand even if the data is accurate.

3.5 Labels and Annotations

The original chart requires readers to estimate most values visually. Our improved version will include selective percentage labels, but only where they help the reader.

Labelling every segment would create clutter. Instead, we will label only dominant categories or values above a selected threshold. This keeps the chart clean while still showing the most important numbers.

We also propose adding short annotations for key patterns, such as:

  • Countries with high oil dependence
  • Countries with high electricity share
  • Countries where industry dominates final energy use
  • Countries with a more diversified fuel mix

These annotations help the visualization tell a clearer story instead of leaving the reader to decode every detail independently.

Although we are redesigning the chart, we preserve several strengths of the original visualization.

4. Data Overview & Quality

4.1 Data Source and Extraction Method

Unlike a typical multi-block spreadsheet export, our data does not originate from a downloadable workbook. It is derived from a single published chart in the International Energy Agency’s Southeast Asia Energy Outlook 2026 report:

Total final consumption mix by fuel and end-use sector in Southeast Asia, 2024 IEA (2026), Paris. https://www.iea.org/data-and-statistics/charts/total-final-consumption-mix-by-fuel-and-end-use-sector-in-southeast-asia-2024, Licence: CC BY 4.0

The chart’s own download page explicitly states: “The data from this chart is not available for download.” Only a chart image (a set of stacked bars, one per country, split by fuel and by end-use sector) is published — there is no underlying machine-readable table to parse. Because of this, the values in energy_data.csv were visually estimated by our team from the published chart, not scraped or downloaded as exact published figures. This is a deliberate methodological choice given the constraint, not an extraction error, and it is the single most important caveat for this section: every percentage in our dataset is an approximation, not an official IEA statistic.

Extraction process:

  • Each country’s stacked bar was read visually and each segment’s length converted to a percentage, rounded to the nearest 0.5 percentage point (the level of precision visually distinguishable on the published chart).
  • Segments were cross-checked to sum to 100% within each country × dimension before being accepted, to catch misreads.
  • The chart covers two dimensions per country — fuel mix and end-use sector mix — both of which we captured.

4.2 Resulting Dataset Structure

Because there is no raw spreadsheet to document the layout of, the “raw” file we work with is the tidy CSV produced by the extraction step above. Its structure is already long-format and rectangular:

Column Type Description
Country character One of 10 ASEAN countries covered by the chart
Dimension character "Fuel" or "Sector" — which breakdown the row belongs to
Category character The specific fuel (e.g. Oil, Coal) or sector (e.g. Industry, Transport)
Percentage double Estimated share of total final consumption, 0–100

There are no metadata rows, no merged headers, and no block separators to skip — read_csv() with default settings parses it correctly. The structural complexity in this dataset sits in the estimation that produced it, not in its file layout.

4.3 Identified Data Quality Challenges

Below is a summary of the data quality challenges identified during the initial data inspection, along with the planned mitigation strategies.

ID Issue Description Severity Planned Decision / Mitigation
C1 Values are visually estimated from a chart rather than sourced from an exact published table. Estimation/reading error of roughly ±1–2 percentage points per segment is possible, and this is irreducible given the source constraint. High Disclose explicitly in the report as a stated limitation. Retain rounding to the nearest 0.5 pp for consistency, and validate that every country’s segments sum to 100% per dimension (already confirmed in our extracted data) as a sanity check rather than a guarantee of accuracy.
C2 The fuel-mix category set is not consistent across countries: Thailand includes an “Other Fuels” category (1.0%) that no other country has a nonzero value for. This reflects the source chart’s shared legend, where small segments are likely omitted from display for most countries rather than genuinely absent. Medium Treat “Other Fuels” as a valid category for all 10 countries, explicitly filling in 0 for countries where it doesn’t currently appear, before pivoting to wide format. Document this assumption in code comments.
C3 The dataset is a single cross-sectional snapshot (2024 only). No time-series comparison is possible from this source alone. Medium Scope the analysis explicitly to a 2024 cross-country comparison; do not attempt trend claims from this dataset. If trend analysis is later required, a second dataset (e.g. the 2015 comparison shown elsewhere in the same IEA report) would need to be separately extracted.
C4 Timor-Leste, ASEAN’s newest member, is absent from the dataset. Low Not an extraction error — the source chart’s own notes state data is unavailable for Timor-Leste. State this explicitly in the report so it reads as a documented source limitation rather than an oversight.

load_energy_data <- function(filepath) {
  energy_data <- read.csv(filepath, stringsAsFactors = FALSE)

  energy_data <- energy_data |>
    dplyr::mutate(
      Country = factor(
        Country,
        levels = c(
          "Brunei Darussalam",
          "Cambodia",
          "Indonesia",
          "Lao DPR",
          "Malaysia",
          "Myanmar",
          "Philippines",
          "Singapore",
          "Thailand",
          "Viet Nam"
        )
      ),
      Dimension = factor(Dimension, levels = c("Fuel", "Sector")),
      Category = factor(Category),
      Percentage = as.numeric(Percentage)
    )

  energy_data
}

5.2 Reproducibility Considerations

To ensure the project is fully reproducible, the following standards are implemented:

  • 📁 File Structure: All raw source assets and intermediate tables are stored in a localized data/ or datasets/ subdirectory within the project root, and file paths are built using relative paths such as data/energy_data.csv so the workflow can be executed unchanged across different host machines.
  • 📦 Package Management: A single setup block at the top of the analysis script loads the required packages in one place, including readr, dplyr, and ggplot2 when available, while sessionInfo() is used at the end of the workflow to preserve an explicit snapshot of the R environment.
  • 🔒 Source File Integrity: Because the dataset was manually reconstructed from an image-based chart, each version of the CSV is preserved with an MD5 checksum and a team verification log records extraction decisions, rounding choices, and disputed values to reduce estimation variance.
  • ✅ Built-in Assertions: The pipeline checks that every country–dimension combination sums to 100%, that Percentage remains numeric, and that the required factor levels for Country, Dimension, and Category are present before any visualisation is rendered.

6. Data Analysis Plan

To answer the three research questions introduced in Section 1.1, the analysis proceeds through three descriptive and structural steps. Each step is derived directly from the long-format energy dataset and uses relative shares rather than absolute volumes, which is consistent with the 100% compositional structure of the proposed visualisation.

  1. Step 1: Resource Dependency Profiling (Fuel Variance Analysis). For each country \(c\), the team will compute the share of each fuel type \(p_{c,f}\) from the cleaned dataset, where \(p_{c,f}\) denotes the percentage share of fuel \(f\) in country \(c\). The dominant fuel dependency for each nation will be identified as \(M_c = \max_f p_{c,f}\), and the associated fuel category will be recorded as \(f_c^* = \arg\max_f p_{c,f}\). Countries exceeding a 50% threshold for a single fuel, such as Oil or Coal, will be flagged as strongly concentrated resource-dependent economies. To complement this threshold-based view, the team will also calculate a concentration measure using a Herfindahl-Hirschman Index, \(HHI_c = \sum_f p_{c,f}^2\), where higher values indicate more concentrated fuel portfolios and lower values indicate greater diversification. This step directly addresses whether the ASEAN countries differ meaningfully in their resource dependencies and whether some economies display a narrow fossil-fuel profile while others are more diversified.

  2. Step 2: Sectoral Demand Disparity Mapping (End-Use Ratios). The second step will focus on the sector dimension by calculating the relative weight of productive industrial demand versus consumer-facing overhead. For each country, the team will compute \(IndustrialShare_c = p_{c,\text{Industry}}\) and \(ConsumerOverhead_c = p_{c,\text{Transport}} + p_{c,\text{Buildings}}\). These two quantities will then be compared using the ratio \(R_c = \frac{IndustrialShare_c}{ConsumerOverhead_c}\). Values of \(R_c > 1\) will indicate economies whose energy demand is disproportionately driven by manufacturing and industrial activity, whereas values below 1 will point to countries where transport and building demand dominate. A complementary difference term, \(\Delta_c = IndustrialShare_c - ConsumerOverhead_c\), will be used to rank countries on a simple productive-versus-consumer scale. This step addresses whether energy demand structures differ systematically across the region and whether some economies are more industrially oriented while others are more residential or service-driven.

  3. Step 3: Macroeconomic Vulnerability Indexing (Risk Convergence). The final step will join the fuel and sector outputs into a single exposure metric that captures the overlap between high oil reliance and inelastic consumer demand. Because direct import-volume data are not available in the chart-derived dataset, the share of Oil will be used as a practical proxy for oil import exposure. The team will therefore compute a vulnerability index for each country as \(V_c = \frac{OilShare_c \times ConsumerOverhead_c}{100}\), where \(OilShare_c\) is the percentage share of oil in the country’s fuel mix and \(ConsumerOverhead_c\) is the combined share of transport and buildings in the sector mix. Higher values of \(V_c\) indicate stronger convergence between externally exposed fuel dependence and essential household or mobility demand, which is precisely the combination that intensifies sensitivity to retail cost-of-living shocks. Countries exceeding both a high oil-share threshold and a high consumer-overhead threshold will be classified as high-risk cases, providing a direct descriptive basis for assessing which ASEAN economies are most exposed to fuel-price volatility and related inflationary pressures.

## 1.1 Our Project’s Aim

Southeast Asia’s rapid economic development has triggered a profound transformation in its regional energy landscape over the past few decades. Rising energy costs, volatile fuel markets, and changing post-COVID grid demands now sit at the center of intense public and macroeconomic debates, as member nations struggle to balance aggressive industrial growth, urban expansion, and household cost-of-living stability. Our project seeks to answer three interrelated questions using the International Energy Agency’s (IEA) 2024 final energy consumption data for Southeast Asia.

**First**, how do final energy consumption mixes by fuel type actually vary across the different ASEAN nations, and what do these distinct portfolios reveal about each country's baseline resource dependencies?

**Second**, are there meaningful structural disparities in how energy demand is distributed across end-use sectors that aggregate regional narratives conceal? The original visualization attempts to depict both fuel types and sector demands simultaneously, but its crowded layout, uniform column spacing, and ambiguous visual proximity make it incredibly difficult to isolate and evaluate these two separate dimensions.

Our improved chart aims to answer all three questions in a single visualization by encoding four dimensions simultaneously: country, data dimension (fuel profile vs. sector demand), consumption category, and percentage share. The redesign moves beyond the original chart’s cluttered, ambiguous layout to offer a clean, side-by-side comparative architecture. # 2. Critical Analysis of Original Visualization

The original IEA visualization, *Total final consumption mix by fuel and end-use sector in Southeast Asia, 2024*, provides a compact overview of how energy consumption differs across Southeast Asian countries. Its main strength is that it compares two related dimensions in one figure: the **fuel mix** of each country and the **end-use sector** where energy is consumed.

However, the chart also has several limitations. While it works well as a broad regional overview, it is less effective for detailed comparison. The critique below focuses on three main areas: clarity, comparability, and visual design.

## 2.1 Clarity and Cognitive Load

The chart attempts to show two different analytical dimensions at the same time.

The fuel panel answers the question: **what type of energy is consumed?** This includes categories such as oil, coal, natural gas, bioenergy, electricity, and other fuels.

The sector panel answers a different question: **where is energy used?** This includes categories such as industry, transport, buildings, and other uses.

Although these two dimensions are related, they are not the same. Placing them side by side in one dense visual forces the reader to switch between two different category systems. This increases cognitive load, especially for readers who are not already familiar with energy terminology.

Another limitation is that the chart is based on percentage shares. This is useful for comparing composition, but it does not show the absolute volume of energy consumed by each country. As a result, a large energy consumer and a smaller energy consumer are given equal visual weight. This makes the chart effective for showing relative mix, but weaker for communicating the overall scale of regional energy demand.

## 2.2 Comparability Challenges

The stacked bar format makes some comparisons difficult. In a stacked bar chart, only the first segment starts from a common baseline. The remaining segments begin at different positions depending on the categories below them. This makes it harder to compare categories that are not aligned to the same starting point.

For example, if a reader wants to compare the share of coal, electricity, or bioenergy across several countries, they must visually estimate segment lengths from different starting positions. This makes the chart useful for a general overview, but less effective for precise cross-country comparison.

The chart also contains many countries and categories. Even though the original ordering provides regional context, it may not support every analytical question. If the goal is to compare fossil fuel reliance, electricity share, or industrial energy demand, sorting countries by a relevant metric would make the pattern clearer.

## 2.3 Visual Design and Accessibility

The chart relies heavily on colour to distinguish categories. This is necessary for a stacked composition chart, but it also creates visual complexity. When many colours are used in a small space, the chart becomes harder to read, especially if some colours are visually similar or if the reader has colour vision deficiencies.

The legend also requires frequent back-and-forth reading. Readers must look at the segment colour, check the legend, return to the chart, and then compare the segment across countries. This slows down interpretation and reduces the chart’s ability to communicate key insights quickly.

Finally, the chart has limited annotation. It shows many patterns, but it does not guide the reader toward the most important ones. For a report or presentation, the visualization would be stronger if it included clearer labels, short callouts, or a sorted structure that highlights the main story.

## 4.1 Data Source and Extraction Method

Unlike a typical multi-block spreadsheet export, our data does not originate from a downloadable workbook. It is derived from a single published chart in the International Energy Agency's **Southeast Asia Energy Outlook 2026** report:

**Total final consumption mix by fuel and end-use sector in Southeast Asia, 2024**
IEA (2026), Paris. https://www.iea.org/data-and-statistics/charts/total-final-consumption-mix-by-fuel-and-end-use-sector-in-southeast-asia-2024, Licence: CC BY 4.0

The chart's own download page explicitly states: *"The data from this chart is not available for download."* Only a chart image (a set of stacked bars, one per country, split by fuel and by end-use sector) is published — there is no underlying machine-readable table to parse. Because of this, the values in `energy_data.csv` were **visually estimated by our team from the published chart**, not scraped or downloaded as exact published figures. This is a deliberate methodological choice given the constraint, not an extraction error, and it is the single most important caveat for this section: **every percentage in our dataset is an approximation, not an official IEA statistic.**

Extraction process:

- Each country's stacked bar was read visually and each segment's length converted to a percentage, rounded to the nearest 0.5 percentage point (the level of precision visually distinguishable on the published chart).
- Segments were cross-checked to sum to 100% within each country × dimension before being accepted, to catch misreads.
- The chart covers two dimensions per country — fuel mix and end-use sector mix — both of which we captured.

To ensure the project is fully reproducible, the following standards are implemented:

- **📁 File Structure:** All raw source assets and intermediate tables are stored in a localized `data/` or `datasets/` subdirectory within the project root, and file paths are built using relative paths such as `data/energy_data.csv` so the workflow can be executed unchanged across different host machines.

# 6. Data Analysis Plan

To answer the three research questions introduced in Section 1.1, the analysis proceeds through three descriptive and structural steps. Each step is derived directly from the long-format energy dataset and uses relative shares rather than absolute volumes, which is consistent with the 100% compositional structure of the proposed visualisation.

1. **Step 1: Resource Dependency Profiling (Fuel Variance Analysis).** For each country $c$, the team will compute the share of each fuel type $p_{c,f}$ from the cleaned dataset, where $p_{c,f}$ denotes the percentage share of fuel $f$ in country $c$. The dominant fuel dependency for each nation will be identified as $M_c = \max_f p_{c,f}$, and the associated fuel category will be recorded as $f_c^* = \arg\max_f p_{c,f}$. Countries exceeding a 50% threshold for a single fuel, such as Oil or Coal, will be flagged as strongly concentrated resource-dependent economies. To complement this threshold-based view, the team will also calculate a concentration measure using a Herfindahl-Hirschman Index, $HHI_c = \sum_f p_{c,f}^2$, where higher values indicate more concentrated fuel portfolios and lower values indicate greater diversification. This step directly addresses whether the ASEAN countries differ meaningfully in their resource dependencies and whether some economies display a narrow fossil-fuel profile while others are more diversified.

2. **Step 2: Sectoral Demand Disparity Mapping (End-Use Ratios).** The second step will focus on the sector dimension by calculating the relative weight of productive industrial demand versus consumer-facing overhead. For each country, the team will compute $IndustrialShare_c = p_{c,\text{Industry}}$ and $ConsumerOverhead_c = p_{c,\text{Transport}} + p_{c,\text{Buildings}}$. These two quantities will then be compared using the ratio $R_c = \frac{IndustrialShare_c}{ConsumerOverhead_c}$. Values of $R_c > 1$ will indicate economies whose energy demand is disproportionately driven by manufacturing and industrial activity, whereas values below 1 will point to countries where transport and building demand dominate. A complementary difference term, $\Delta_c = IndustrialShare_c - ConsumerOverhead_c$, will be used to rank countries on a simple productive-versus-consumer scale. This step addresses whether energy demand structures differ systematically across the region and whether some economies are more industrially oriented while others are more residential or service-driven.

3. **Step 3: Macroeconomic Vulnerability Indexing (Risk Convergence).** The final step will join the fuel and sector outputs into a single exposure metric that captures the overlap between high oil reliance and inelastic consumer demand. Because direct import-volume data are not available in the chart-derived dataset, the share of Oil will be used as a practical proxy for oil import exposure. The team will therefore compute a vulnerability index for each country as $V_c = \frac{OilShare_c \times ConsumerOverhead_c}{100}$, where $OilShare_c$ is the percentage share of oil in the country’s fuel mix and $ConsumerOverhead_c$ is the combined share of transport and buildings in the sector mix. Higher values of $V_c$ indicate stronger convergence between externally exposed fuel dependence and essential household or mobility demand, which is precisely the combination that intensifies sensitivity to retail cost-of-living shocks. Countries exceeding both a high oil-share threshold and a high consumer-overhead threshold will be classified as high-risk cases, providing a direct descriptive basis for assessing which ASEAN economies are most exposed to fuel-price volatility and related inflationary pressures.
